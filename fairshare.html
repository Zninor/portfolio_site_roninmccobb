FairShare

FaireShare is a native application designed to help groups of users add multiple purchases to an overall bill and divide the cost amongst all users evenly with little hassle. With this app, users can individually pay for big purchases like restaurants, rideshare trips, and shared living costs and know they will only pay their fair share.

In order to further define the app and its Minimum Viable Product, I developed three user objectives to focus on.

  • Create and add friends on the app to a budget.
  • Submit a purchase you made to a budget, to be later divided amongst all users.
  • Pay a "finished" budget that the host has closed, sending or receiving money based on your purchases and division of cost.

After deciding on the main user objectives I would work on, I began drawing a user flow to decide what screens were necessary and how they should flow from one to another. Here, you can see the three tasks in a combined user flow to show how one would navigate the app.

With the user flow ready, I began sketching low fidelity wireframes to get a sense of what the screens should look like to achieve the user objectives I laid out. With some minor edits, I then transferred these to Adobe XD as mid-fidelity wireframes

As I began designing the mid-fidelity wireframes, I realized my user flow did not quite make sense in how users created and paid for budgets. Now, instead of paying money for a newly found budget and withdrawing funds later, purchases are tallied until the host declares the budget is closed, then money is paid and dispersed amongst users.

With a new user flow, some wireframes also had to be thought out differently. The Withdraw and Proof-of-Purchase page are replaced by an Add-Purchase page (where users post a purchase they've made for the budget, thus increasing the budget), a separate Payment page for once the entire budget is deemed completed, and a new Receipt page is added to show users who must pay what amount.

Once I completed the greyscale version of the app, I began working on a style guide to find the right typography, color palette, and UI pattern design.

The original color for FairShare was meant to be orange - I wanted to stand out from other money apps like Venmo and Paypal that use blue, so I chose a bright, fun, and unique color palette.

After a quick feedback round, I realized white and orange did not pair as well when it came to legibility.

I decided to change to green: it's bright and pairs well with white, plus it symbolizes money so better fitting for FairShare while still unique from other money apps.

A small feedback round yielded better results: less harsh on the eyes while still remaining bright and eye-catching.

I then applied the finished style guide to my mid-fidelity screens to create a finished version of FaireShare, one I could share with others as a potential finished product.

Retrospective

How did I interpret the original project brief?

The project brief was very open ended: create an app that will solve a user problem. I have often been the person amongst friends in college doing the bulk of group purchasing, and having to chase down individuals to pay me the $8.50 they owe me, often to no avail. I wanted to create something that would work to make that problem easier by keeping a running tally of purchases that everyone can see, along with proof of purchase so everyone feels like things are fair, and ease the burden of whoever is making purchases for the group.

What insights did I gain from testing?

I learned from testers that having the default iOS/Android font everywhere did not look good, even though I thought it was necessary to include it since this was native application. After changing the font to a more unique and bolder design, the overall design looked much better. I was able to still maintain the default font for body text, so that it feels comfortable for users still.

What roadblocks did I encounter?

As someone who has used an iPhone their entire life, Android design and style was completely new for me. It was difficult at first to determine how I should change my designs to fit the Android Style Manual, and I made many drastic differences that I later learned were unnecessary. Overtime, I realized it was more subtle differences, such as font, button style, and shadowing that really made the Android screens look the way they should, and I look forward to getting more experience with Android design now that I have been introduced to it.
